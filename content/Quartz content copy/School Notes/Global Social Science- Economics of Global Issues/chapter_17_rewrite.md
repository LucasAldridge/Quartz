# 1. What America Trades & With Whom

## A. The Big Picture
Since the 1970s, the U.S. has become extremely globalized:
- Exports ≈ **10% of U.S. GDP**
- Imports ≈ **13% of U.S. GDP**

Imports rise faster → **U.S. trade deficit**.

# 2. Why Trade Makes Both Sides Better Off

Most people think:
> “If Mexico wins, the U.S. loses.”

Economics says:
> “Trade increases total world production.”

## A. Absolute Advantage
A country has **absolute advantage** if it produces more with the same resources.

## B. Comparative Advantage (the real key)
A country has **comparative advantage** if it gives up *less* to produce one good compared to another.

### How to find comparative advantage (simple steps)
1. Compute opportunity cost of producing one unit of a good → (what you give up).  
2. Compare across countries.  
3. The country with the **lower** opportunity cost has comparative advantage.

### Example Table:
| Country | Coffee | Apples |
|---------|--------|---------|
| U.S. | 3 | 2 |
| Brazil | 2 | 1 |

### Opportunity costs:
**U.S.:**
- 1 coffee → 2/3 apples  
- 1 apple → 3/2 coffee  

**Brazil:**
- 1 coffee → 1/2 apple  
- 1 apple → 2 coffee  

### Comparative advantage:
- Coffee: **Brazil**  
- Apples: **United States**

Even if the U.S. is better at *both*, trade is still beneficial.

# 3. How Trade Expands Total Consumption
Using a **production possibilities frontier (PPF)**:

Without trade → each country is stuck on its own PPF  
With specialization → world production increases  
With trade → both countries consume **beyond** their own PPFs

# 4. How Tariffs Alter Supply & Demand

Tariffs act like a **tax** on imports.

### Without trade:
- Domestic price = high  
- Domestic producers supply more  
- Consumers buy less  

### With world trade:
- Price falls to world price  
- Domestic producers supply less  
- Consumers buy more  
- Total welfare increases  

### With a tariff:
Tariff raises the import price → supply curve shifts left → price rises.

Effects:
- Domestic output rises  
- Domestic consumption falls  
- Imports shrink  
- Government collects tariff revenue  
- **Deadweight loss** appears (total welfare lower)

# 5. What Dumping Actually Is

Dumping =  
**A foreign company selling goods in the U.S. below production cost**  
OR  
**selling cheaper abroad than at home**  
(usually to kill U.S. competitors)

Purpose:
- Drive competitors out of business  
- Gain monopoly → raise prices later  

Real-world issue:
- Hard to prove actual production costs  
- Rarely works long-term  
- Often used as a political accusation

# 6. Who Gets Hurt by Trade
Trade increases total wealth but redistributes jobs.

## Losers:
- Workers in industries losing comparative advantage  
- Towns dependent on a single factory  
- Low-skill workers forced into retraining  
- Workers who get new jobs but with lower wages  

## Why jobs move:
- Cheaper labor abroad  
- Automation  
- Offshoring  
- Efficiency improvements in foreign markets

# 7. Valid Reasons to Limit Trade

## A. National Security
Weapons, sensitive tech → must be domestic.

## B. National Identity
Japan protects rice because it’s culturally foundational.

## C. Harmful comparative advantage
Countries may have low costs due to:
- child labor  
- poor treatment of workers  
- pollution  
- environmental destruction  

Agreements like USMCA & WTO create enforcement standards.

---

# 8. Methods of Limiting Trade

## A. Tariffs
Taxes on imports → raise price.

## B. Quotas
Limits on quantity imported → raises price but no tax revenue.

## C. Nontariff Barriers
Regulations, inspections, certifications that make importing too costly.

# 9. Trade as a Diplomatic Weapon
Examples of sanctions used as political pressure:
- Cuba  
- Iran

- North Korea

- Iraq  

**Effectiveness:** Low

---

# 10. The Cost of Protectionism
Economists measured how much it costs to “save” one job with tariffs.

Results:
- Some industries cost **$942,000 per job saved**  
- Average cost ≈ **$300,000 per job saved** (inflation-adjusted)

Protectionism is extremely expensive.

---

# Final Summary
- America imports more than it exports → large deficits  
- Comparative advantage → main reason trade increases wealth  
- Tariffs raise prices, reduce imports, and cause deadweight loss  
- Dumping = selling below cost to eliminate competition  
- Trade helps the economy overall but hurts certain workers  
- Protectionist policies save few jobs at extremely high cost  
- Good reasons for limits: security, culture, ethics  
- Trade as diplomacy rarely works
