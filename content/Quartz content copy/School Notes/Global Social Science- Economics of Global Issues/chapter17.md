	 # Chapter 17 — International Trade (Lucas-Style Summary)

## What This Chapter Is Really About
International trade = countries buying/selling stuff with each other.  
People argue trade “steals jobs,” but economics says trade usually makes everyone better off, even though some industries and workers get hurt short-term.

---

# 1. What the U.S. Trades + With Whom

## Major U.S. Imports
- Electronics (computers, TVs, phones)
- Steel
- Oil
- Cars

## Major U.S. Exports
- Airplanes
- Financial services (insurance, banking)
- Machinery
- Chemicals
- Education & medical services (international students, medical care)

## Why we import AND export the same categories
- Broad product categories (cars, chemicals)
- Brand preferences (BMW vs Cadillac)

---

# 2. Why Countries Trade

Trade works because countries benefit from focusing on what they’re relatively best at.

### Absolute Advantage
A country is simply *better* at producing something.

### Comparative Advantage (the key idea)
A country has a *lower opportunity cost* in producing something.

Even if one country is better at producing everything, trade can still benefit both sides when they specialize according to comparative advantage.

---

# 3. How Trade Makes Everyone Better Off

When countries specialize:
- Total world production increases.
- Both countries can consume more than before.
- Each country produces what it sacrifices the least to make.

Think group projects:
Everyone does the part they’re best at → better total output.

---

# 4. Who Gets Hurt by Trade

Even when the country benefits overall:
- Some industries shrink.
- Some workers lose jobs and need retraining.
- Short-term pain happens before long-term gains.

But job turnover is normal:
- ~30 million U.S. jobs are eliminated yearly.
- ~31 million new jobs are created yearly.

Most job loss is due to technology, not trade.

---

# 5. Reasons People Want to Limit Trade

## Weak/Questionable Reasons
- Protecting jobs (short-term gain, long-term harm)
- Claims of “dumping”
- Failing industries wanting protection from competition

## Good/Economically Valid Reasons
- **National security** (we wouldn’t buy fighter jets from enemies)
- **National identity** (Japan protecting rice)
- **Ethical concerns**  
  - Pollution  
  - Unsafe working conditions  
  - Child labor

---

# 6. Methods for Limiting Trade

## A. Tariffs
A tax on imports; raises the price of foreign goods.  
Examples: U.S. tariffs on steel, aluminum, and scotch.

## B. Quotas
Limits the *quantity* of imports.  
Importers profit; government receives no tax revenue.

## C. Non-Tariff Barriers
Rules/inspections that make importing difficult or expensive.

Examples:
- Disease inspections on food
- EU restrictions on GMO crops
- Japanese restrictions on U.S. apples

---

# 7. Using Trade as a Diplomatic Weapon (Sanctions)

Countries try to punish others by cutting off trade.  
Examples: Cuba, Iran, USSR, Libya, Iraq, North Korea, Russia.

**Reality:**  
Sanctions almost never force countries to change behavior.  
They simply trade with someone else.

---

# 8. Costs of Protectionism

Protectionism:
- Raises prices for consumers
- Reduces total economic welfare
- Saves jobs at extremely high cost

Example costs per job saved:
- Textiles: ~$148,000  
- Machinery: ~$348,000  
- Chemicals: ~$942,000  

Protectionism = paying hundreds of thousands to save a job that may pay ~$60k.

---

# Final Summary (Quick Version)

- Trade increases total production and lowers prices.
- Everyone benefits overall, but some workers/industries get hurt short-term.
- Valid trade limits: security, identity, ethical issues.
- Tariffs, quotas, and rules restrict trade.
- Sanctions rarely work.
- Protectionism costs society a lot and is economically inefficient.
