# 1. Why This Matters

International trade isn’t just about moving goods (like iPhones).  
It’s also about moving **money**, *currencies*, and *assets* across borders.

To trade globally, countries must:
- exchange currencies,
- buy/sell each other’s assets,
- balance inflows and outflows of money.

This chapter explains:
- how international financial flows work,
- how exchange rates are determined,
- and why currencies get stronger or weaker.

---

# 2. International Financial Transactions

Whenever the U.S. buys foreign goods (like an iPhone built in China), money leaves the U.S.  
But **that money must eventually return** to the U.S. somehow.

If $100,000,000 leaves the U.S., the holder of those dollars can:

- Buy U.S. goods/services  
- Buy U.S. financial assets (Treasuries, stocks)  
- Buy U.S. physical assets (land, buildings, factories)  
- Hold the dollars (rare unless for safety or illegal activity)

This is tracked using the **Balance of Payments (BoP)**—the accounting system of all money flows between the U.S. and the world.

---

# 3. The Balance of Payments (BoP)

The BoP has three major parts:

## **A. Current Account**
Includes:
- Trade in goods (exports/imports)
- Income from investments
- Taxes paid across borders
- Money transfers (remittances)

The U.S. has:
- a massive trade deficit  
- → therefore a massive **current account deficit**

## **B. Capital Account**
Includes:
- People who move countries and take assets
- Transfers of intellectual property
- Gift/inheritance taxes internationally

This account is usually very small.

## **C. Financial Account**
Tracks buying/selling of:
- stocks
- bonds
- real estate
- businesses
- other long-term assets

When foreigners buy U.S. assets, the financial account is **positive**.

### **Key relationship:**
If the U.S. runs a current account deficit →  
it must run a **financial account surplus**, because the dollars returning = foreign investment into the U.S.

# 4. Foreign Exchange Markets (How Currencies Get Traded)

When a U.S. buyer pays for an imported product:
- the seller needs their own currency
- currencies must be exchanged

Example:  
Apple pays Foxconn (China) → Foxconn needs **yuan**, not dollars → dollars must be exchanged into yuan.

### **Important principle:**
Demand for yuan = supply of dollars  
Demand for dollars = supply of yuan

Exchange rate = *price of one currency in terms of another.*

Example:  
- “USD per yuan”  
- or “yuan per USD”  
Both are just reciprocals.

---

# 5. Types of Foreign Exchange Systems

There are **three major systems** for determining exchange rates.

---

## **A. Floating Exchange Rate**
- No government control.
- Supply & demand set the currency price.
- Most major world currencies use this system today.

---

## **B. Fixed Exchange Rate**
- Government keeps its currency at a set value.
- To maintain it, the government must:
  - Buy its own currency when demand is low  
  - Sell its own currency when demand is high  

Example:  
China used a nearly fixed exchange rate for years.

---

## **C. Managed Float (Dirty Float)**
- Currency mostly floats
- BUT government steps in if it leaves an acceptable range (the “ceiling” or the “floor”)

China uses this today:
- Yuan typically kept between 6 and 7 yuan per dollar
- Government intervenes only when it breaks out of the desired range

---

# 6. What Makes Exchange Rates Move (Determinants)

Currencies get stronger or weaker based on **relative conditions** between two countries.

---

## **1. Trade Imbalances**
If a country imports more than it exports:
- more of its currency flows abroad  
- → its currency weakens (in a pure market system)

U.S.–China example:
- U.S. runs huge trade deficits → dollar would normally weaken vs yuan  
- but China often controls the exchange rate, so market forces don’t act fully.

---

## **2. Real Interest Rate Differences**
Real interest rate = nominal interest rate – inflation expectations

Investors chase the highest return.  
So:
- Higher real interest rate → stronger currency  
- Lower real interest rate → weaker currency

If inflation differs:
- The country with lower expected inflation → stronger currency

---

## **3. Safety of Assets**
In global crises, investors want **safe assets**.

The U.S. dollar is the world’s safest currency because:
- U.S. government always pays its debts
- Large, stable economy
- Most global contracts are USD-based

This means:
- During 9/11  
- During the 2008 financial crisis  
- During COVID  

→ the dollar **strengthened**, even when the crisis started in the U.S.

---

# **Final Summary**

- Money flows globally because of trade and investment.  
- Exchange rates represent the “price” of one currency in terms of another.  
- Currencies can float, be fixed, or be managed.  
- Currency values change because of:
  - trade imbalances  
  - interest rates  
  - inflation expectations  
  - asset safety  
- The U.S. dollar tends to strengthen during global instability.
