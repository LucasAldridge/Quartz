# 1. Big Idea of This Chapter

Trade agreements (NAFTA/USMCA, CAFTA, GATT, WTO, TPP) exist because:
- Free trade usually makes countries richer overall  
- But countries are tempted to cheat (tariffs, quotas)  
- Agreements stop trade wars and keep the system stable  

Most economists argue:
**Free trade = more total wealth, higher living standards, and stronger long-term growth.**

But free trade also creates:
- Job losses in some industries  
- Wage inequality  
- Environmental and labor concerns  

This chapter breaks down all of that.

---

# 2. The Logic Behind Free Trade

Most non-economists think trade works like:
> “If Mexico wins, the U.S. loses.”

Economists think:
> “If each country makes what it’s best at making, total output rises for everyone.”

Example (U.S. vs Mexico, aircraft vs shoes):
- U.S. workers are better at everything  
- But Mexico has relatively more advantage in shoes  
- U.S. has more advantage in aircraft  

If both countries specialize:
- Total world output increases  
- Both countries end up with more goods  
- Workers displaced in shoe industries in the U.S. move to higher-skill jobs

# 3. Why Trade Agreements Are Needed

Even if free trade is beneficial:
Countries are tempted to cheat by:
- Imposing tariffs  
- Setting quotas  
- Subsidizing their industries  

## A. Strategic Trade (Economic Motivation)
Countries sometimes try to boost their industries with subsidies/tariffs:
- Example: Airbus vs Boeing  
- France & Britain subsidized Airbus heavily  
- Gave them an edge even though subsidies > profits  

If every country tried this → global trade collapses.

## B. Special Interests (Political Motivation)
Groups that lose jobs push hard for protectionism:
- Manufacturing workers  
- Unions  
- Towns hurt by plant closures  

But groups benefiting from trade often *don’t realize they benefit*:
- Consumers  
- Export-oriented industries  
- Farmers  
- Businesses  

So political pressure is uneven.

## C. What Agreements Prevent
If the U.S. imposes tariffs and Mexico doesn’t retaliate → U.S. gains in the short run.  
But Mexico loses more than the U.S. gains → total global welfare drops.

If both retaliate → trade war → everyone loses.

**Trade agreements stop this spiral.**

# 4. Trade Agreements & Institutions

## A. GATT (General Agreement on Tariffs & Trade)
- Established rules for tariffs & retaliation  
- Made trade conflicts structured instead of chaotic  

Key rules:
- Retaliation must be proportional  
- Copyright & intellectual property protections strengthened  
- Movie/TV distribution rules debated but unresolved  

## B. WTO (World Trade Organization)
- Created to resolve trade disputes  
- Has no enforcement power, but can declare who is right/wrong  
- Introduced formal mechanisms for conflict resolution  

## C. NAFTA → USMCA
NAFTA (1994) created free trade zone for U.S., Canada, Mexico.  
USMCA (2020) slightly modified NAFTA:
- More U.S. dairy exports to Canada  
- Stronger copyright protections  
- Cars built in Mexico must use at least 40% U.S.-made parts  

---

# 5. Are These Agreements Actually Working?

### Trade Growth
Trade between U.S., Mexico, and Canada increased—but was already increasing before NAFTA.

### Job & Wage Impacts
Economists disagree:
- Some say NAFTA increased U.S. jobs slightly  
- Others say 2 million manufacturing jobs were lost  

Wages:
- Manufacturing wages fell (1970–2015)  
- High-skill wages rose sharply  
- Inequality increased  

Industries hit hardest:
- Autos  
- Steel  
- Electronics  
- Textiles  

These industries once provided high-paying low-skill jobs. Their decline hit workers without college degrees hardest.

# 6. Economic & Political Concerns About Trade Agreements

## A. Lower Wages for U.S. Workers
Companies may move production abroad if:
- Wages abroad are much lower  
- Savings outweigh shipping costs  

Result:
- Some workers lose jobs  
- Others get new jobs but earn less  
- Professionals and skilled workers benefit the most  

## B. Unfair Labor Practices Abroad
Concerns include:
- Child labor  
- Abuse of workers  
- No worker protections  
- Extremely low wages  
- Unsafe conditions  

These practices make foreign firms _unfairly_ competitive.

## C. Weak Environmental Standards
Example: Mexican maquiladoras (pre-NAFTA)
- Produced toxic waste  
- Weak treatment of industrial wastewater  
- Pollution flowed into the U.S. (Rio Grande)

Trade agreements can:
- Require improving standards over time  
- Encourage countries to follow global labor/environmental treaties  

---

# 7. The Bottom Line (Economists’ View)

Most economists support trade agreements because:
1. People should be free to buy/sell across borders unless it harms others.  
2. Carefully written trade agreements increase global economic well-being.  

Trade:
- Eliminates some jobs  
- Creates more jobs overall  
- Helps poor countries grow  
- Helps rich countries specialize in high-skill industries  

BUT:
- Losers must be helped (retraining, income support)  
- Otherwise trade becomes “rich get richer, poor get poorer”  

Economists also note:
- Automation + computers are reducing low-skill jobs even without trade  
- Workers need higher education and retraining to compete
